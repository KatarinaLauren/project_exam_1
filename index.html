A SpaceX timeline

1971

Elon Musk is born

◢

✖

1971

Elon Musk

  • Was born 28 June 1971 in Pretoria, South Africa
  • Became a multi millionaire in his late 20s
  • 1999 Founder of X.com - later PayPal
  • 2002 Founder and CEO of SpaceX
  • 2003 Co-founder and CEO of Tesla Motors
  • One of the most well-known people on the planet today

2002

SpaceX is founded

◢

✖

2002

Space Exploration Technologies Corp

The ultimate goal of SpaceX is reducing space transportation costs to eventually enable the colonization of Mars.

Elon Musk calculated that the material cost of building a rocket at the time was about 3% of the actual sales price of a rocket. He saw huge potential to lower this cost and still make a good profit.

On 6th of May 2002, SpaceX was founded.

SpaceX homepage

2005

Falcon 1 development

◢

✖

2005

Development and testing of Falcon 1

SpaceX begins testing Falcon 1, its first real rocket design created with the goal of reusable space flight.

November 25th 2005 the first Falcon 1 launch is attempted.

The first launch failed

SpaceX launches SpaceX rockets

2006-2007

Falcon 1 failures

◢

✖

2006-2007

SpaceX continues to test and try and launch the Falcon 1 rocket. All attempted launches fail.

Despite the failures, SpaceX still manages to win a NASA contract and secure additional funding for the company.

In 2007 SpaceX also moves to Hawthorne, California, where they are still located today.

Want to know more about how rockets work? Visit National Geographic for a good explanation on the topic.

2008

First launch success

◢

✖

2008

The first successful launch of Falcon 1!

After the third Falcon 1 failure in August 2008 where 3 satellites where lost, the future of SpaceX was looking less bright and they were in need of a win.

On 28th of September 2008 a milestone mission was carried out.

The fourth launch of Falcon 1 was a success.

Falcon 1 becomes the first privately-developed liquid-fuel launch vehicle to go into orbit around the Earth.

2009

First satellite delivered

◢

✖

2009

First satellite delivered to Earth orbit

On 14th of July 2009 Falcon 1 has its fifth flight and becomes the first privately developed liquid fuel rocket to deliver a commercial satellite to Earth orbit.

This Malaysian satellite was the first and only commercial contract launch of Falcon 1.

In 2009 Elon Musk also joins Twitter, where he goes on to become a notable figure.

2010

Falcon 9 first flight

◢

✖

2010

First Falcon 9 flight

The Falcon 9 rocket has been developed since 2005 and the first launch was initially planned to be in the second half of 2007.

The plans were delayed and the Falcon 9 rocket flew for the first time in June 2010.

Today, Falcon 9 has the most launches among all U.S. rockets currently in operation. Rockets from the Falcon 9 family have been launched 100 times over the past 10 years and has a mission success rate of 98%

SpaceX launches SpaceX rockets

2010

Recovery from orbit

◢

✖

2010

Spacecraft recovered from orbit

In December 2010 the Falcon 9 rocket flew for the second time, this time together with a fully functioning Dragon capsule.

The rocket returns, which makes SpaceX the first privately funded company to successfully launch, orbit, and recover a spacecraft.

This important test mission brings SpaceX closer to the goal of creating fully reusable rockets.

Visit SpaceX and read more about the Dragon capsule

2011

Grasshopper program

◢

✖

2011

The Grasshopper program is launched

The Grasshopper program's mission was to develop reusable rockets that would be able to safely and fully functionally land back on Earth after going to space.

SpaceX did many Grasshopper tests with rockets launching into the air and landing again. The first 24 storey test flight was completed in March 2013.

2012

First mission to the ISS

◢

✖

2012

The first successful mission to the ISS (International Space Station)

SpaceX becomes the first private company to send a spacecraft to the ISS (Falcon 9 Flight 3). The launch is the company's second demonstration test flight for NASA's Commercial Orbital Transportation Services (COTS) Program.

SpaceX Dragon commercial cargo craft is grappled by the Canadarm2 robotic arm at the International Space Station

2013

Satellite into geosynchronous orbit

◢

✖

2013

First private company to send a satellite into geosynchronous orbit

Falcon 9 rocket launches a commercial communications satellite into orbit. The mission is SpaceX's first commercial satellite launch into a geostationary transfer orbit

Geosynchronous equatorial orbit (GEO)

is a low inclination orbit around Earth having a period of 23 hours 56 minutes 4 seconds. A spacecraft in geosynchronous orbit appears to remain above Earth at a constant longitude, although it may seem to wander north and south. The spacecraft returns to the same point in the sky at the same time each day.

2014

NASA contract to fly astronauts

◢

✖

2014

NASA contract to fly astronauts

NASA awards SpaceX a US$2.6 billion contract to fly American astronauts, and announces that SpaceX and Boeing will be the two companies developing spacecraft to send astronauts to the International Space Station.

SpaceX’s crew capsule is called the Dragon V2.

2015

Failed landing on a drone ship

◢

✖

2015

Attempted landing on spaceport drone ship

In January 2010 SpaceX begins a series of first stage landing attempts of its Falcon 9 rocket on an autonomous spaceport drone ship. The rocket crashes and burns.

An autonomous spaceport drone ship (ASDS) is an ocean-going vessel derived from a deck barge, outfitted with station-keeping engines and a large landing platform and is controlled by an autonomous robot.

SpaceX currently has two operational drone ships, Just Read the Instructions (II) and Of Course I Still Love You, both of which operate in the Atlantic for launches from Cape Canaveral.

2015

Rocket explosion

◢

✖

2015

A rocket explodes during takeoff

In June 2015 SpaceX was supposed to carry out yet another resupply mission to the ISS.The Falcon 9 rocket explodes during takeoff.

This was the seventh resupply mission to ISS that SpaceX was supposed to carry out as part of their contract for NASA, the previous six missions had all been succesful.

2015

Milestone landing on land

◢

✖

2015

A mission milestone

In December 2015 the Falcon 9 flight 20 launches carrying 11 Orbcomm-OG2 communications satellites to Earth orbit.

In this mission SpaceX achieves the first landing of an orbital rocket's first stage on land.

2016

Landing on ocean platform

◢

✖

2016

Successful landing on ocean platform

In April 2016 SpaceX achieves the first landing of an orbital rocket's first stage on an ocean platform

After boosting the payload on its orbital trajectory, the rocket's first stage re-entered the denser layers of the atmosphere and landed vertically on the ocean landing platform Of Course I Still Love You nine minutes after liftoff.

Thus achieving a long-sought-after milestone in SpaceX reusable launch system development program.

2016

Falcon 9 explosion

◢

✖

2016

Another Falcon 9 explosion

Throughout 2016 SpaceX carries out 8 succesful missions for both satellite deliveries and ISS resupply.

In September 2016 a mission fails as the Falcon 9 explodes on the pad, just before a static-fire test.

Israeli Earth communications satellite Amos-6 is destroyed in the explosion

2017

First reuse of a rocket

◢

✖

2017

First time reuse of rocket

In March 2017 the Falcon 9 launches from NASA’s Kennedy Space Center in Florida with the SES-10 communications satellite.

The rocket was the first to ever be reused and launched for a second time.

This was also the first rocket to land intact for a second time.

2017

Reflight of cargo spacecraft

◢

✖

2017

First time reflight of cargo spacecraft

In June 2017 SpaceX carries out another resupply mission to the ISS.

This mission launches as the first reflight of a commercial cargo spacecraft, the SpaceX Dragon C106.

It is the first time the same unmanned spacecraft returns to the space station.

The ISS (International Space Station)

2017

Big Falcon Rocket BFR

◢

✖

2017

SpaceX announces updated rocket design.

In September 2017 at the 68th annual meeting of the International Astronautical Congress, SpaceX unveils the updated vehicle design of a big Falcon rocket.

Musk says, "we are searching for the right name, but the code name, at least, is BFR.

BFR stands for Big Falcon Rocket, but was informally also called Big Fucking Rocket.

BFR has eventually evolved into the spacecraft Starship.

A prototype of the BFR

2018

Elon Musk sends his Tesla to space

◢

✖

2018

Elon Musk sends his Tesla to space.

In February 2018 SpaceX successfully conducts a test launch of its Falcon Heavy rocket carrying aboard a Tesla Roadster and “Starman”, a dummy in a SpaceX spacesuit.

The Roadster is destined into a billion-year orbit around Mars.

Visit Wikipedia and read more about the Tesla stunt

The Falcon Heavy has the highest payload capacity of any currently operational launch vehicle, and the third-highest capacity of any rocket ever to reach orbit.

SpaceX rockets

2018

50th launch and new contract

◢

✖

2018

50th launch and new contract

In March 2018 a Falcon 9 rocket is launched for the 50th time, successfully orbiting a city bus-sized satellite for Spanish operator Hispasat.

The United States Air Force announces a deal with SpaceX to fly three of the newest generation of Global Positioning System satellites into space, at an average cost of US$97 million per flight.

2018

First contract for BFR

◢

✖

2018

First contract for BFR

SpaceX reports having signed its first customer to fly on the company’s new rocket, the Big Falcon Rocket (BFR).

Days later SpaceX reveals that the customer is Yusaku Maezawa, a Japanese billionaire and founder of Zozotown, Japan’s largest online clothing retailer.

Maezawa will be the first commercial passenger to do a flyby around the Moon and the flight is slated to take place no earlier than 2023 with a duration of nearly six days.

2019

The Starship

◢

✖

2019

First test of the Starship

A test version of SpaceX’s next-generation spacecraft, the Starship, successfully ignites its onboard engine for the first time.

SpaceX’s Starship spacecraft and Super Heavy rocket represent a fully reusable transportation system designed to carry both crew and cargo to Earth orbit, the Moon, Mars and beyond.

In July 2019 the Starship (Starhopper) launches for the first time. The rocket makes its first free-flying test launch at SpaceX's Boca Chica proving ground in South Texas.

2019

SpaceX valuation

◢

✖

2019

SpaceX valuation

In May 2019 SpaceX valuation rises to US$33.3 billion, thus surpassing Tesla, Inc.

2019

Starhopper final test

◢

✖

2019

Starhopper final test

SpaceX Starhopper, the first prototype for its Mars-colonizing Starship vehicle, is put to final test flight, rising several hundred feet off the ground at the SpaceX South Texas Launch Site in Boca Chica Village, Texas.

Starhopper reaches a hover altitude and then flies sideways to touch down at a separate nearby landing pad. The entire flight lasts just 57 seconds.

2020

Starship tests

◢

✖

2020

Starship tests

In February 2020 SpaceX Starship SN1 prototype explodes during a pressure test.

In April 2020 SpaceX Starship SN4 becomes the first full-scale prototype to pass and survive a cryogenic proof test, in which the ship’s normal liquid oxygen and methane is replaced with similarly frigid but non-explosive liquid nitrogen.

100 successful flights

On October 24th SpaceX celebrated its 100th successful misson.

2020

First crewed test flight

◢

✖

2020

First crewed test flight of a Crew Dragon spacecraft

Crew Dragon's first non-piloted test flight occurred in March 2019, and its first crewed flight – with astronauts Robert Behnken and Douglas Hurley – occurred in May 2020.

This test flight marked the first time a private company launched a crewed orbital spacecraft.

2020

SpaceX crew-1 - first crewed mission

◢

✖

2020

First crewed mission of a Crew Dragon spacecraft

The Crew Dragon spacecraft Resilience launched on 16 November 2020 at 00:27:17 UTC on a Falcon 9 from the Kennedy Space Center.

The spacecraft carried NASA astronauts Michael Hopkins, Victor Glover and Shannon Walker along with JAXA astronaut Soichi Noguchi,

The mission is expected to last 180 days, meaning the flight will return to Earth sometime around June 2021.

The first crewed mission was originally planned to take place in 2012.
