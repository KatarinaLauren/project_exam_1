2002

SpaceX is founded

2002

Space Exploration Technologies Corp

The ultimate goal of SpaceX is reducing space transportation costs to eventually enable the colonization of Mars.

Elon Musk calculated that the material cost of building a rocket at the time was about 3% of the actual sales price of a rocket. He saw huge potential to lower this cost and still make a good profit.

On 6th of May 2002, SpaceX was founded.

2005

Falcon 1

2005

Development and testing of Falcon 1

SpaceX begins testing Falcon 1, its first real rocket design created with the goal of reusable space flight.

November 25th 2005 the first Falcon 1 launch is attempted.

The first launch failed

SpaceX launches SpaceX rockets

2006-2007

Falcon 1 failures

2006-2007

SpaceX continues to test and try and launch the Falcon 1 rocket. All attempted launches fail.

Despite the failures, SpaceX still manages to win a NASA contract and secure additional funding for the company.

In 2007 SpaceX also moves to Hawthorne, California, where they are still located today.

2008

First launch success

2008

The first successful launch of Falcon 1!

After the third Falcon 1 failure in August 2008 where 3 satellites where lost, the future of SpaceX was looking less bright and they were in need of a win.

On 28th of September 2008 a milestone mission was carried out.

The fourth launch of Falcon 1 was a success.

Falcon 1 becomes the first privately-developed liquid-fuel launch vehicle to go into orbit around the Earth.

2009

First satellite delivered

2009

First satellite delivered to Earth orbit

On 14th of July 2009 Falcon 1 has its fifth flight and becomes the first privately developed liquid fuel rocket to deliver a commercial satellite to Earth orbit.

This Malaysian satellite was the first and only commercial contract launch of Falcon 1.

In 2009 Elon Musk also joins Twitter, where he goes on to become a notable figure.

2010

Falcon 9

2010

First Falcon 9 flight

The Falcon 9 rocket has been developed since 2005 and the first launch was initially planned to be in the second half of 2007.

The plans were delayed and the Falcon 9 rocket flew for the first time in June 2010.

Today, Falcon 9 has the most launches among all U.S. rockets currently in operation. Rockets from the Falcon 9 family have been launched 100 times over the past 10 years and has a mission success rate of 98%

SpaceX launches SpaceX rockets

2010

Recovery from orbit

2010

Spacecraft recovered from orbit

In December 2010 the Falcon 9 rocket flew for the second time, this time together with a fully functioning Dragon capsule.

The rocket returns, which makes SpaceX the first privately funded company to successfully launch, orbit, and recover a spacecraft.

This important test mission brings SpaceX closer to the goal of creating fully reusable rockets.

Visit SpaceX and read more about the Dragon capsule

2011

Grasshopper program

2011

The Grasshopper program is launched

The Grasshopper program's mission was to develop reusable rockets that would be able to safely and fully functionally land back on Earth after going to space.

SpaceX did many Grasshopper tests with rockets launching into the air and landing again. The first 24 storey test flight was completed in March 2013.

2012

First mission to the ISS

2012

The first successful mission to the ISS (International Space Station)

SpaceX becomes the first private company to send a spacecraft to the ISS (Falcon 9 Flight 3). The launch is the company's second demonstration test flight for NASA's Commercial Orbital Transportation Services (COTS) Program.

SpaceX Dragon commercial cargo craft is grappled by the Canadarm2 robotic arm at the International Space Station

2013

Satellite into geosynchronous orbit

2013

First private company to send a satellite into geosynchronous orbit

Falcon 9 rocket launches a commercial communications satellite into orbit. The mission is SpaceX's first commercial satellite launch into a geostationary transfer orbit

Geosynchronous equatorial orbit (GEO)

is a low inclination orbit around Earth having a period of 23 hours 56 minutes 4 seconds. A spacecraft in geosynchronous orbit appears to remain above Earth at a constant longitude, although it may seem to wander north and south. The spacecraft returns to the same point in the sky at the same time each day.

2014

NASA contract to fly astronauts

2014

NASA contract to fly astronauts

NASA awards SpaceX a US$2.6 billion contract to fly American astronauts, and announces that SpaceX and Boeing will be the two companies developing spacecraft to send astronauts to the International Space Station.

SpaceX’s crew capsule is called the Dragon V2.
